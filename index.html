    Motivation

    • The SIRIUS's Storage Ring needs sub-micrometer orbit stability to maintain its performance
    • The orbit stability depends on the precision and accuracy of the Beam Position Monitors (BPM)
    • The BPMs have high precision (nanometer-level) but low accuracy
    • So we calibrate the BPMs using the Beam-Based Alignment (BBA) procedure

    The Problem

    The current BBA procedure is very time-consuming (6∼8 hours for all 160 BPMs)

    Theoretical Background

    Coordinate System

    We adopt a curvilinear coordinate system based on the Frenet-Serret frame, that follows a design orbit $\vec{r}_0(s)$

    $\vec{v}$
    $\vec{r}_0$
    $\hat{x}$
    $\hat{y}$
    $\hat{s}$
    • $\hat{s}(s) = \dfrac{d}{ds}\vec{r}_0(s)$
    • $\hat{x}(s) = -\rho(s)\dfrac{d}{ds}\hat{s}(s)$
    • $\hat{y}(s) = \hat{s}(s) \times \hat{x}(s)$

    For high energy particles ($v \rightarrow c$), their state is described in terms of small deviations from the design orbit

    Magnetic Fields

    In the Frenet-Serret frame, the transverse magnetic fields can be multipole-expanded around the design orbit

    $B_y(x,y,s) + i B_x(x,y,s) = \sum \Big( B_n(s) + iA_n(s) \Big)(x+iy)^n$

    Dipole:
    $$\Biggl\{ \begin{array}{l} B_x = 0 \\ B_y = B_0 \end{array} \Biggr.$$
    Quadrupole:
    $$\Biggl\{ \begin{array}{l} B_x = B_1 y \\ B_y = B_1 x \end{array} \Biggr.$$
    Skew Quadrupole:
    $$\Biggl\{ \begin{array}{l} B_x = A_1 x\\ B_y = -A_1 y \end{array} \Biggr.$$

    Magnetic Fields

    We normalize the field coefficients by the magnetic rigidity \(B\rho\), defining the magnetic lattice functions

    Dipole:

    $G(s) \equiv \dfrac{B_0(s)}{B\rho} = \dfrac{1}{\rho(s)}$

    (Curvature function)

    Quadrupole:

    $K(s) \equiv \dfrac{B_1(s)}{B\rho}$

    (Focusing function)

    Skew Quadrupole:

    $K_S(s) \equiv \dfrac{A_1(s)}{B\rho}$

    (Skew quad. strength)

    Transverse Dynamics

    From the Hamiltonian formalism, we derive the equations of motion

    $$\left\{ \begin{array}{l} x'' = -\dfrac{(1 + Gx)^2}{1 + \delta}\dfrac{B_y}{B\rho} + G(1 + Gx) \\ y'' = +\dfrac{(1 + Gx)^2}{1 + \delta}\dfrac{B_x}{B\rho} \end{array} \right.$$

    where $\delta = \frac{p - p_0}{p_0} \approx \frac{E - E_0}{E_0}$

    In the linear approximation:

    $$\Biggl\{ \begin{array}{l} x'' = -\left(K + G^2\right)x + G\delta + K_S y \\ y'' = + K y + K_S x \end{array} \Biggr.$$

    Betatron Motion

    For on-energy particles, the equations of motion reduce to the Hill's equation

    $$u'' = -K_u u$$

    where $u = x, y$, $\ K_x = K + G^2$ and $\ K_y = -K.$

    The solution gives $$u(s)= a\sqrt{\beta_u(s)} \cos\!\left(\phi_u(s)-\phi_0\right)$$

    which we call the betatron oscillations

    Betatron Motion

    $$u(s) = a\sqrt{\beta_u(s)} \cos\!\left(\phi_u(s)-\phi_0\right)$$

    The betatron functions satisfy $$\dfrac{1}{2}\beta'' + K(s)\beta = \dfrac{1}{\beta}\left[1 + \left(\dfrac{\beta'}{2}\right)^2\right] \qquad \Biggl\{\begin{array}{rcl} \beta(s) &=& \beta(s+L) \\ \beta'(s) &=& \beta'(s + L)\end{array}\Bigg.$$

    and we define the phase-advance and tune $$\phi_u(s) = \int\limits_0^s \dfrac{1}{\beta_u(s')} ds' \qquad \nu_u(s) = \dfrac{1}{2\pi}\int\limits_s^{s+L} \dfrac{1}{\beta_u(s')} ds'$$

    Dispersion

    For off-energy particles, the horizontal motion depends on a particular solution

    $$ x'' = -(K + G^2) x + G\delta$$

    → we choose $x_\delta(s) = \eta(s)\delta$

    The solution describes an off-energy orbit associated to the dispersion function $\eta$, which satisfies $$\eta'' = -\left(K+G^2\right)\eta + G \qquad \Biggl\{\begin{array}{rcl} \eta(s) &=& \eta(s+L) \\ \eta'(s) &=& \eta'(s + L)\end{array}\Bigg.$$

    Closed Orbit

    In an ideal machine the closed orbit is the design orbit, in which particles travel around

    → However, dipolar field imperfections ($\delta G$) distort the closed orbit

    $$u_{co}(s) = \dfrac{\sqrt{\beta_u(s)}}{2\sin{\pi\nu}}\int\limits_s^{s+L} \delta G_u(s')\sqrt{\beta_u(s')}\cos{\left(|\phi_u(s) - \phi_u(s')| - \pi\nu_u\right)}\Delta s'$$

    To ensure the accelerator performs as designed, is essential to control and correct the closed orbit

    Orbit Feedback-System

    In the SIRIUS Storage Ring, we have a combination of sensors, actuators and algorithms to control the orbit

    • Beam Position Monitors
    • Corrector Magnets
    • FOFB and SOFB

    Orbit Feedback-System: Beam Position Monitors

    • 160 BPMs to read the transverse beam position

    Two pair of antennas that convert current signal into transverse beam position

    $$x = \zeta\left(\dfrac{A - C}{A + C} - \dfrac{B - D}{B + D}\right)$$

    $$y = \zeta\left(\dfrac{B - D}{B + D} + \dfrac{A - C}{A + C}\right)$$

    where $\zeta = 8.5$ mm is a calibration factor

    → Resolution: $\approx$2 nm

    Orbit Feedback-System: Correctors

    • 120 CH (slow horizontal correctors)
    • 160 CV (slow vertical correctors)
    • 80 FCH (fast horizontal correctors)
    • 80 FCV (fast vertical correctors)

    Orbit Feedback-System: Orbit Correction

    As dipolar field imperfections, localized dipolar kicks also change the closed orbit $$\Delta u(s_i) = \dfrac{\sqrt{\beta_u(s_i)}}{2\sin{\pi\nu_u}}\sum \Delta\theta_j\sqrt{\beta_u(s_j)}\cos{\left(|\phi_u(s_i) - \phi_u(s_j)| - \pi\nu_u\right)} $$

    $\Delta\vec{u} = \mathbf{M}\Delta\vec{\theta}$

    where

    $\vec{u} = ( x_1, \dots , x_{\text{\small N}_\text{BPM}}, y_1, \dots , y_{\text{\small N}_\text{BPM}} )$

    $\vec{\theta} = ( \theta_{1}^x, \dots ,\theta_{\text{\small N}_\text{CH}}^x, \theta_{1}^y, \dots ,\theta_{\text{\small N}_\text{CV}}^y )$

    and $\mathbf{M}$ is the Orbit Response Matrix

    Orbit Feedback-System: Orbit Correction

    $$\Delta\vec{u} = \mathbf{M}\Delta\vec{\theta}$$

    Lets say we read an orbit distortion $\vec{u}_0$ on the machine, we need $\vec{u}_0 + \Delta \vec{u} = 0$

    → $\vec{u}_0 + \mathbf{M}\Delta\vec{\theta} = 0$

    → $\Delta \vec{\theta} = -\mathbf{M}^{-1}\vec{u}_0$

    giving us the necessary kicks $\Delta\vec{\theta}$ to correct the distortion

    Orbit Feedback System: SOFB and FOFB

    SOFB

    • Slow Orbit FeedBack
    • @ 10 Hz

    • 120 CH + 160 CV + 1 RF
    • Compensates slow drifts

    Orbit Feedback System: the accuracy problem

    The electric center of BPMs does not necessarily match the design orbit

    Natural imperfections and misalignments also make the BPMs inaccurate

    → To solve this, we use Beam-Based Alignment techniques
    to calibrate the BPMs

    Beam-Based Alignment

    The BBA Principle

    The calibration of BPMs consists of finding the offsets that indicate whether the beam travels on an "ideal" orbit defined by the magnetic centers of the magnets on the accelerator

    The BBA Principle

    From the EOM, we know that a beam passing off-center on a quadrupole will experience a kick and disturb the orbit

    $$\Delta x' = -KL\, x$$

    This makes quadrupoles the most suitable magnets for BBA, since the orbit distortion, beam position and quadrupole strength are linearly related

    The BBA Principle

    The distance between a BPM and a quadrupole is crucial for determining the offset accurately

    • farther the BPM → higher the difference between $x_\text{offset, BBA}$ and $x_\text{offset, real}$
    • closer the BPM → $x_\text{offset, BBA} \approx x_\text{offset, real}$

    The BBA Principle

    For a BPM-Quad pair close enough $$x_\text{quad} \approx (x_\text{bpm} - x_\text{offset, real}) \approx (x_\text{bpm} - x_\text{offset, BBA})$$

    This way $$\Delta x' \approx -KL\, (x - x_\text{offset})$$

    From this we determine the offset by varying the beam position and the quadrupole strength

    → If the orbit does not change when varying $K$, then $x = x_\text{offset}$

    Standard BBA

    The standard BBA method emerged in the 80s and was widely discussed in the 90s with seminal works

    • R. Schmidt, "Misalignments from K-modulation" (CERN SL/93-19, 1993)
    • P. Röjsel, "A Beam Position Measurement System Using Quadrupole Magnets Magnetic Centra as the Position Reference" (NIM, A 343, 1994)
    • Barnett et al., "Dynamic Beam Based Alignment" (AIP 333, 1995)

    The method empirically applies the BBA principle by modulating the strength of quadrupoles and scanning the orbit on the BPMs to find the offsets

    → In the SIRIUS Storage Ring, the method was studied and implemented in 2019–2020 (commissioning)

    Standard BBA

    The standard BBA procedure consists of:

    • $\rightarrow$ Scan the transverse plane on a BPM: $\mathbf{r}_{i} = (x_i, y_i)$
    • $\qquad\rightarrow$ For each $\mathbf{r}_i$, vary the quadrupole strength: $K^\pm = K_0 \pm \Delta K$
    • $\qquad\qquad\rightarrow$ For each $K^\pm$, collect the orbit on all BPMs: $\vec{u}^\pm_i$
    • $\qquad\qquad\qquad\rightarrow$ Calculate the orbit distortion: $\Delta \vec{u}_i = \frac{1}{2}(\vec{u}^+_i - \vec{u}^-_i)$
    • $\rightarrow$ Fit $\Delta\vec{u}_i = \vec{a}\, \mathbf{r}_i + \vec{b}$ or $\chi_i^2 = a\, \mathbf{r}_i^2 +b\, \mathbf{r}_i + c$ where $\chi^2_i = \dfrac{1}{N_\text{\small BPMs}}\sum\limits_{j=0}^{N_\text{\small BPMs}} (\Delta u_{i,j})^2$
    • $\rightarrow$ Solve for $\Delta\vec{u} = 0$: $\mathbf{r}_\text{offset} = \left\langle\dfrac{-\vec{b}}{\vec{a}}\right\rangle$ or $\dfrac{\partial\chi^2}{\partial\mathbf{r}} = 0$: $\mathbf{r}_\text{offset} = \left(\dfrac{-b}{2a}\right)$

    Parallel BBA

    The Parallel Beam Beam Alignment (PBBA) was proposed by Xiaobiao Huang in 2022

    Since then, it has been simulated and tested on accelerators such as

    • LCLS-II (USA, SLAC, 2022)
    • SPEAR3 (USA, SLAC, 2022)
    • FCC-ee (Switzerland, CERN, 2023/2024)
    • KARA (Germany, KIT, 2024/2025)
    • NSLS-II (USA, BNL, 2024)

    Parallel BBA

    • The central idea is to minimize the Induced Orbit Shift (IOS)

    → Our goal is to find an orbit that satisfies: $$\vec{u}(\vec{\theta}, \vec{K}_0) = \vec{u}(\vec{\theta}, \vec{K}^\pm)$$

    → The IOS is defined as: $$\vec{\xi} = \frac{1}{2}\left(\vec{u}(\vec{\theta}, \vec{K}^+) - \vec{u}(\vec{\theta}, \vec{K}^-)\right)$$

    → Minimizing the IOS results in: $$\vec{\xi} = 0 \Leftrightarrow \vec{u}(\vec{\theta}, \vec{K}^+) = \vec{u}(\vec{\theta}, \vec{K}^-) \Leftrightarrow \vec{u}(\vec{\theta}, \vec{K}_0) = \vec{u}(\vec{\theta}, \vec{K}^\pm)$$

    Parallel BBA

    → Linearizing $\vec{\xi}$ around an initial kick configuration $\vec{\theta}_0$ gives: $$\vec{\xi}\ \ =\ \ \vec{\xi}(\vec{\theta_0}) + \dfrac{\partial \vec{\xi}}{\partial \vec{\theta}}(\vec{\theta} - \vec{\theta_0}) \ \ =\ \ \vec{\xi}_0 + \mathbf{R}\Delta\vec{\theta}$$

    where $\mathbf{R}_{ij} = \partial \vec{\xi}_i/\partial \vec{\theta}_j$ is the IOS Response Matrix

    → Solving for $\vec{\xi} = 0$ we get: $$\Delta\vec{\theta} = -\mathbf{R}^{-1}\vec{\xi}_0$$

    $\vec{\theta} = \vec{\theta_0} + \Delta\vec{\theta} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \vec{u}_\text{offset} = \vec{u}(\vec{\theta}, \vec{K}_0) = \vec{u}(\vec{\theta}, \vec{K}^\pm)$

    Parallel BBA

    The Parallel BBA procedure consists of:

    • $\rightarrow$ Separate all BPM-Quad pairs into groups
    • $\rightarrow$ Define $\vec{K}^+$ and $\vec{K}^-$ for each group
    • $\rightarrow$ Get the IOS Response Matrix $\mathbf{R}$ for each group
    • $\rightarrow$ For each group, do:
    • $\qquad\rightarrow$ Loop while $\vec{\xi} \neq 0$
    • $\qquad\qquad\rightarrow$ Measure the IOS: $\vec{\xi} = \frac{1}{2}\left(\vec{u}(\vec{\theta}, K^+) - \vec{u}(\vec{\theta}, K^-)\right)$
    • $\qquad\qquad\rightarrow$ Compute the delta kicks ($\Delta\vec{\theta} = -\mathbf{R}^{-1}\vec{\xi})$ and apply ($\vec{\theta} = \vec{\theta} + \Delta\vec{\theta}$)
    • $\qquad\rightarrow$ Read and save the offsets: $\vec{u}_\text{offset} = \vec{u}(\vec{\theta}, \vec{K}_0) = \vec{u}(\vec{\theta}, \vec{K}^\pm)$

    Fast BBA AC

    The Fast Beam Beam Alignment with AC excitations (FBBA-AC) was proposed by Zeus Martí (et al.) in 2020

    Since then, it has been simulated and tested on accelerators such as

    • ALBA (Spain, CELLS, 2020)
    • Diamond (UK, DLS, 2023-2024)
    • HEPS (China, IHEP, 2023)
    • HALF (China, NSRL, 2025)

    Fast BBA AC

    The Fast BBA AC is a robust improvement of the standard BBA

    → Instead of discrete orbit shifts, AC excitations on the correctors are used to induce the orbit distortions.

    → High acquisition rate BPMs are used to collect the orbit data

    → The linear relationship between orbit distortions and quadrupole modulation is extracted with harmonic analysis to obtain the BPMs offsets

    Fast BBA AC

    The FBBA-AC procedure consists of:

    • $\rightarrow$ For each BPM-Quad pair:
    • $\qquad\rightarrow$ For each quadrupole strength $K^-$ and $K^+$:
    • $\qquad\qquad\rightarrow$ Apply sinusoidal AC excitations to suitable CH and/or CV
    • $\qquad\qquad\rightarrow$ Acquire fast BPM data during the excitation
    • $\qquad\rightarrow$ Correlate the orbit distortions to calculate the BPM offsets

    Parallel BBA: Partial Conclusion

    Until now PBBA has shown:

    • High speed — the execution time is 45∼90 times faster (6 hours to 4∼8 minutes)
    • Accuracy — comparisons with Standard BBA show differences of a few microns
    • Repeatability — PBBA runs under the same configuration differ by a few microns
