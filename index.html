    The Problem

    The Storage Ring orbit must be controlled with micrometer-level stability to preserve the ultra-low emittance and deliver high-quality synchrotron light to the beamlines.[1][2]

    Beam Position Monitors (BPMs) provide sub-micrometer resolution for relative position changes, but their absolute accuracy is only at the level of hundreds of micrometers, which is insufficient for 4th-generation tolerances.[1]

    The standard Beam-Based Alignment (BBA) procedure used at SIRIUS is accurate but sequential and slow, taking about 6-8 hours to calibrate all 160 BPMs in the Storage Ring.[1]

    • High number of BPM-quadrupole pairs to calibrate (~160 BPMs).[1]
    • Long machine time required for traditional BBA (operational bottleneck).[1]
    • Need to maintain or improve measurement accuracy while reducing execution time and human intervention.[1]

    Objectives

    Main Goal

    To study, validate and implement alternative Beam-Based Alignment techniques for BPM calibration in the SIRIUS Storage Ring, reducing calibration time while preserving accuracy and precision.[1]

    Specific Objectives

    • Study the traditional BBA procedure and its implementation at SIRIUS.[1]
    • Review and understand alternative BBA methods proposed in the literature (parallel and AC-based techniques).[1]
    • Simulate selected alternative BBA schemes using SIRIUS models (pyaccel, pymodels, apsuite).[1]
    • Implement, test and experimentally validate at least one alternative BBA method at SIRIUS.[1]

    Theoretical Background

    Synchrotron Main Components

    Bending Magnets

    Dipole (bending) magnets generate a nearly uniform vertical magnetic field that bends the trajectory of electrons in the horizontal plane, defining the closed design orbit.[3][2]

    The magnetic rigidity and the dipole field fix the nominal energy of the Storage Ring through the curvature function $G(s)$:

    $G(s) = \dfrac{1}{\rho(s)} = \dfrac{ec}{E_0} B_0(s)$.

    • Dipoles are the main source of horizontal dispersion, since off-energy particles follow orbits with different lengths.[3][2]
    • In a perfectly planar ring, dipoles should not generate vertical dispersion; misalignments and coupling lead to residual vertical dispersion.[2]

    Focusing Magnets

    Quadrupole magnets provide linear focusing in one transverse plane and defocusing in the orthogonal plane, acting as magnetic lenses for the beam.[3][2][1]

    Their strength is described by the focusing function $K(s)$, proportional to the transverse gradient of the magnetic field:

    $K(s) = \frac{ec}{E_0} \left.\frac{\partial B_y}{\partial x}\right|_{x=0} = \frac{ec}{E_0} \left.\frac{\partial B_x}{\partial y}\right|_{y=0}$.[1][2]

    • Linear transverse dynamics (no energy deviation) reduce to Hill's equations, with solutions described by betatron functions and phase advance.[2][1]
    • Quadrupole strength modulation is the core knob used in BBA techniques, since orbit changes depend on the beam offset inside the quadrupole.[1]

    Coordinate System

    Accelerator physics adopts a curvilinear Frenet-Serret frame following the design orbit, and the state of a particle is written as:

    $\mathbf{x} = (x, x', y, y', \delta, z)^T$.[3][2][1]

    • $x, y$: transverse displacements with respect to the design orbit.
    • $x', y'$: conjugate momenta, approximated as transverse angles for ultra-relativistic beams.
    • $\delta$: relative momentum (or energy) deviation.
    • $z$: longitudinal path length deviation.

    Transverse Dynamics

    In the linear approximation and in the presence of curvature $G(s)$ and focusing $K(s)$, the transverse motion obeys:[2][1]

    $x'' = -(K + G^2)\, x + G\, \delta,$
    $y'' = K\, y.$

    For on-energy particles ($\delta = 0$), the dynamics reduce to Hill’s equation and the general solution can be written as:

    $u(s) = \dfrac{a}{\sqrt{\beta_u(s)}} \cos(\phi_u(s) - \phi_0), \quad u = x, y.$[2][1]

    When energy deviation is included, the particular solution for the horizontal plane introduces the dispersion function $\eta(s)$, via:

    $x_\delta(s) = \eta(s)\, \delta,\quad \eta'' = -(K + G^2) \eta + G.$[3][2][1]

    Dispersion is a key optical function for understanding orbit distortions due to energy spread, and vertical dispersion control at SIRIUS has been studied and corrected through dedicated methods (QS, CV).[2]

    Maps Formulation

    In tracking codes such as Trackcpp, accelerators are modeled as ordered sequences of elements, each represented by a transfer map acting on the 6D state vector.[3]

    A simple drift of length $L_d$ is represented by the map:

    $x \leftarrow x_0 + x'_0 L_d,\quad x' \leftarrow x'_0,$
    $y \leftarrow y_0 + y'_0 L_d,\quad y' \leftarrow y'_0,$
    $\delta \leftarrow \delta_0,\quad z \leftarrow z_0 + \dfrac{L_d}{2}\dfrac{x'^2_0 + y'^2_0}{(1+\delta_0)^2}.$[3]

    The one-turn map $M$ is the composition of all element maps along the ring:

    $M = M_N \circ M_{N-1} \circ \dots \circ M_1,\quad \mathbf{x}_{n+1} = M(\mathbf{x}_n).$[3]

    The closed orbit $\mathbf{x}^*$ is the fixed point of the one-turn map, satisfying $M(\mathbf{x}^*) = \mathbf{x}^*$; numerically, it is obtained using iterative methods such as Newton-Raphson applied to the 6D map.[3]

    Beam-Based Alignment Techniques

    Standard BBA

    In the traditional BBA, the goal is to determine the BPM offset relative to the magnetic center of the nearest quadrupole, using the beam itself as a probe.[1]

    For each BPM-quadrupole pair $i$, the beam is steered to a set of transverse positions $(x_i, y_i)$ around the nominal orbit:

    $x_i = x_{i0} + n\, \Delta x,\quad y_i = y_{i0} + m\, \Delta y,\quad n, m = 0,1,2,\dots$[1]

    For each beam position, the quadrupole strength is modulated around its nominal value:

    $K = K_0 \pm \Delta K,$[1]

    and the induced orbit change is measured in all BPMs:

    $\Delta \text{orb}_x = \text{orb}_x(+\Delta K) - \text{orb}_x(-\Delta K),$
    $\Delta \text{orb}_y = \text{orb}_y(+\Delta K) - \text{orb}_y(-\Delta K).$[1]

    The BPM offset is obtained from the beam position where the induced orbit change vanishes, i.e. where the beam passes exactly through the quadrupole magnetic center:[1]

    $x_{i,\text{offset}} = x_i\ \big|\ \Delta\text{orb}_x = 0,\quad y_{i,\text{offset}} = y_i\ \big|\ \Delta\text{orb}_y = 0.$[1]

    Although very accurate and conceptually simple, this serial procedure must be repeated for all BPM-quadrupole pairs and multiple beam positions, resulting in long calibration times at SIRIUS.[1]

    Parallel BBA

    Parallel BBA techniques aim at measuring the alignment of multiple quadrupoles simultaneously by correcting or analyzing the orbit response to combined quadrupole strength variations.[1]

    An example is the “Simultaneous beam-based alignment measurement for multiple magnets by correcting induced orbit shift”, where orbit distortions induced by quadrupole modulation are corrected using steering magnets, and the required corrector strengths encode the quadrupole-BPM offsets.[1]

    • Exploits global orbit response instead of scanning one quadrupole at a time.[1]
    • Potentially reduces total measurement time and allows for more automated procedures.[1]
    • Requires careful modeling of optics and corrector/BPM systems, and robust data analysis (e.g., SVD-based inversion).[2][1]

    Fast AC BBA

    Fast AC BBA techniques use time-varying (usually sinusoidal) excitation of quadrupole strengths and synchronous detection of the beam response at BPMs to extract offsets.[1]

    Instead of stepping $K$ to $K_0 \pm \Delta K$ quasi-statically, a small AC modulation is applied around the nominal strength, and the resulting orbit oscillation at the modulation frequency is measured.[1]

    • Frequency-domain analysis improves signal-to-noise ratio and allows faster data acquisition.[1]
    • AC methods can be combined with parallel schemes to obtain multi-magnet BBA with reduced machine time.[1]
    • Implementation at SIRIUS must consider hardware limits, available modulation channels and impact on stored beam stability.[2][1]

    Experiments

    Experimental Setup

    The main experimental system is the SIRIUS Storage Ring, with its orbit correction system, quadrupole families and 160 BPMs distributed along the ring.[2][1]

    The FAC group tools used for modeling and online control include pyaccel, pymodels, apsuite and siriuspy, which provide optics calculations, orbit and optics correction and high-level machine control interfaces.[3][2][1]

    • Orbit steering system and vertical/horizontal correctors.[2]
    • Quadrupole families with programmable strengths for BBA modulation.[2][1]
    • BPM system capable of acquiring orbit data during BBA procedures with sub-micrometer resolution.[2][1]
    • Data analysis scripts for orbit response, SVD and fitting of BBA signals (in analogy with tools like Pynel used in dispersion studies).[3][2]

    Preliminary Results

    Previous work at LNLS has successfully implemented and validated advanced optics correction techniques (e.g., vertical dispersion correction using QS and CV methods) and longitudinal dynamics improvements in Trackcpp, demonstrating the feasibility of combining modeling, simulations and machine experiments.[3][2]

    These experiences provide the methodological basis for the current project, where Parallel and fast BBA techniques are being implemented and tested at SIRIUS.[3][2][1]

    • First PBBA implementation completed and tested in a machine experiment on 13/10/2025.[1]
    • Initial analysis shows consistency between model predictions and measured orbit responses, but detailed performance evaluation (time reduction, robustness) is ongoing.[1]
    • Further experiments in 2026 will refine the method and assess whether additional BBA techniques (e.g., Fast AC BBA) need to be implemented.[1]

    Next Steps

    Conclusion

    The SIRIUS Storage Ring requires precise and efficient BPM calibration to fully exploit its 4th-generation performance, motivating the study of alternative Beam-Based Alignment techniques beyond the traditional, time-consuming serial method.[2][1]

    Building on previous experience with optics correction and advanced simulation tools at LNLS, this project combines theoretical modeling, numerical simulations and machine experiments to validate Parallel and fast BBA schemes tailored to the SIRIUS environment.[3][2][1]

    The expected outcome is a robust BBA procedure that significantly reduces calibration time while preserving or improving BPM accuracy and reproducibility, contributing directly to SIRIUS orbit control and beam quality.[1]
