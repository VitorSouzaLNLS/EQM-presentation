Magnetic Field Representation

In the Frenet-Serret frame, the transverse magnetic field can be expanded locally around the design orbit using a multipole representation

General form

$$B_y(x,y,s) + i B_x(x,y,s) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \left(B_n(s) + iA_n(s)\right)(x+iy)^n$$

  • \(B_n\): normal multipole coefficients
  • \(A_n\): skew multipole coefficients
  • \(n=0\): dipole, \(n=1\): quadrupole, \(n=2\): sextupole

Dipoles provide bending, quadrupoles provide linear focusing, and sextupoles introduce the leading nonlinear correction

Lowest-Order Multipoles

The first terms of the multipole expansion correspond to the magnetic elements most relevant for linear optics and chromatic correction

Dipole:
$$\left\{ \begin{array}{l} B_x = 0 \\ B_y = B_0 \end{array} \right.$$
Quadrupole:
$$\left\{ \begin{array}{l} B_x = B_1 y \\ B_y = B_1 x \end{array} \right.$$
Sextupole:
$$\left\{ \begin{array}{l} B_x = 2 B_2 x y \\ B_y = B_2 (x^2 - y^2) \end{array} \right.$$

In what follows, these field coefficients are rewritten in terms of the normalized strengths \(G(s)\), \(K(s)\) and \(S(s)\)

Normalized Magnetic Strengths

For beam dynamics, it is convenient to normalize the magnetic field coefficients by the magnetic rigidity \(B\rho\)

$$G(s) \equiv \frac{B_0(s)}{B\rho} = \frac{1}{\rho(s)}$$

$$K(s) \equiv \frac{B_1(s)}{B\rho}$$

$$S(s) \equiv \frac{B_2(s)}{B\rho}$$

  • \(G(s)\): reference curvature set by the dipoles
  • \(K(s)\): quadrupole focusing strength
  • \(S(s)\): sextupole strength

These normalized functions are the quantities that naturally appear in the transverse equations of motion

Linear Transverse Dynamics

In the paraxial and linear approximation, the transverse motion is governed by the curvature and quadrupole focusing functions

$$x'' = -\left(K + G^2\right)x + G\delta$$

$$y'' = K y$$

  • The horizontal plane includes the effect of curvature
  • The quadrupoles define the linear focusing structure
  • For \(\delta = 0\), the homogeneous motion reduces to Hill's equation

Betatron Motion and Dispersion

For on-energy particles \((\delta = 0)\), the dynamics reduce to Hill's equation and the solution can be written as

$$u(s)=\frac{a}{\sqrt{\beta_u(s)}} \cos\!\left(\phi_u(s)-\phi_0\right), \qquad u=x,y$$

For off-momentum particles, the horizontal motion includes a particular solution associated with the dispersion function

$$x(s)=x_\beta(s)+\eta(s)\delta$$

$$\eta'' = -\left(K+G^2\right)\eta + G$$

The optical functions \(\beta(s)\), \(\phi(s)\) and \(\eta(s)\) determine the orbit response and are central to beam-based alignment analyses

Experimental Setup

The main experimental system is the SIRIUS Storage Ring, with its orbit correction system, quadrupole families and 160 BPMs distributed along the ring.[2][1]

The FAC group tools used for modeling and online control include pyaccel, pymodels, apsuite and siriuspy, which provide optics calculations, orbit and optics correction and high-level machine control interfaces.[3][2][1]

  • Orbit steering system and vertical/horizontal correctors.[2]
  • Quadrupole families with programmable strengths for BBA modulation.[2][1]
  • BPM system capable of acquiring orbit data during BBA procedures with sub-micrometer resolution.[2][1]
  • Data analysis scripts for orbit response, SVD and fitting of BBA signals (in analogy with tools like Pynel used in dispersion studies).[3][2]

Preliminary Results

Previrimenous work at LNLS has successfully implemented and validated advanced optics correction techniques (e.g., vertical dispersion correction using QS and CV methods) and longitudinal dynamics improvements in Trackcpp, demonstrating the feasibility of combining modeling, simulations and machine expets.[3][2]

These experiences provide the methodological basis for the current project, where Parallel and fast BBA techniques are being implemented and tested at SIRIUS.[3][2][1]

  • First PBBA implementation completed and tested in a machine experiment on 13/10/2025.[1]
  • Initial analysis shows consistency between model predictions and measured orbit responses, but detailed performance evaluation (time reduction, robustness) is ongoing.[1]
  • Further experiments in 2026 will refine the method and assess whether additional BBA techniques (e.g., Fast AC BBA) need to be implemented.[1]

Standard BBA

In the traditional BBA, the goal is to determine the BPM offset relative to the magnetic center of the nearest quadrupole, using the beam itself as a probe.[1]

For each BPM-quadrupole pair $i$, the beam is steered to a set of transverse positions $(x_i, y_i)$ around the nominal orbit:

$x_i = x_{i0} + n\, \Delta x,\quad y_i = y_{i0} + m\, \Delta y,\quad n, m = 0,1,2,\dots$[1]

For each beam position, the quadrupole strength is modulated around its nominal value:

$K = K_0 \pm \Delta K,$[1]

and the induced orbit change is measured in all BPMs:

$\Delta \text{orb}_x = \text{orb}_x(+\Delta K) - \text{orb}_x(-\Delta K),$
$\Delta \text{orb}_y = \text{orb}_y(+\Delta K) - \text{orb}_y(-\Delta K).$[1]

The BPM offset is obtained from the beam position where the induced orbit change vanishes, i.e. where the beam passes exactly through the quadrupole magnetic center:[1]

$x_{i,\text{offset}} = x_i\ \big|\ \Delta\text{orb}_x = 0,\quad y_{i,\text{offset}} = y_i\ \big|\ \Delta\text{orb}_y = 0.$[1]

Although very accurate and conceptually simple, this serial procedure must be repeated for all BPM-quadrupole pairs and multiple beam positions, resulting in long calibration times at SIRIUS.[1]

Parallel BBA

Parallel BBA techniques aim at measuring the alignment of multiple quadrupoles simultaneously by correcting or analyzing the orbit response to combined quadrupole strength variations.[1]

An example is the “Simultaneous beam-based alignment measurement for multiple magnets by correcting induced orbit shift”, where orbit distortions induced by quadrupole modulation are corrected using steering magnets, and the required corrector strengths encode the quadrupole-BPM offsets.[1]

  • Exploits global orbit response instead of scanning one quadrupole at a time.[1]
  • Potentially reduces total measurement time and allows for more automated procedures.[1]
  • Requires careful modeling of optics and corrector/BPM systems, and robust data analysis (e.g., SVD-based inversion).[2][1]

Fast AC BBA

Fast AC BBA techniques use time-varying (usually sinusoidal) excitation of quadrupole strengths and synchronous detection of the beam response at BPMs to extract offsets.[1]

Instead of stepping $K$ to $K_0 \pm \Delta K$ quasi-statically, a small AC modulation is applied around the nominal strength, and the resulting orbit oscillation at the modulation frequency is measured.[1]

  • Frequency-domain analysis improves signal-to-noise ratio and allows faster data acquisition.[1]
  • AC methods can be combined with parallel schemes to obtain multi-magnet BBA with reduced machine time.[1]
  • Implementation at SIRIUS must consider hardware limits, available modulation channels and impact on stored beam stability.[2][1]
